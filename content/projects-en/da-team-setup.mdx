## Approach

### 1) Structured Client Needs into Analysis Topic Taxonomy

Client requests were numerous but there were no organizational criteria for what to analyze first.
- Collected analysis-related needs from client meeting records, Slack conversations, and sales requests — organizing 300+ total requests.
- Rather than dividing requests by client/function, classified them along 2 axes matching actual client questioning patterns: phenomenon identification and cause analysis.
- Created the mAsh analysis topic table based on this classification, sharing it for use as common language in product discussions.

As a result, scattered client requests were organized into an analysis topic taxonomy functioning as both team backlog and product discussion criteria.

### 2) Transitioned Team Operations to Process-Based

The initial team was small enough for verbal coordination and ad-hoc collaboration, but consistency in operations would likely break down as the team grew.
- Directly facilitated 20+ sprint retrospectives, continuously recording the team's workflow, blockers, and next experiment tasks.
- Used retrospectives not as simple review meetings but as operational records accumulating what work the team actually repeats and what roles they've taken on.
- Later used as evidence for DA team role clarification discussions.

Team operational context accumulated in documents and records, enabling maintenance of team continuity and improvement rhythm.

### 3) Designed DA Team as Scalable Functional Organization, Not Simple Report Team

In early startups, analytics organizations easily get consumed as support teams that only respond to incoming requests, risking long-term role ambiguity.
- Designed the team's role to include not just client request response but analysis topic structuring, documentation, operational rhythm, and technology experiments.
- In particular, while running LLM experiments and insight automation reviews in parallel, set direction for the DA team to be one that can respond to future technology changes, not just process current analysis work.
- Placed more weight on defining what problems the team continuously solves rather than short-term deliverables.

The DA team began establishing itself not as a one-off analysis support organization but as an independent functional team connecting product and client understanding.
