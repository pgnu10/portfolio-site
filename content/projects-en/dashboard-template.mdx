## Approach

### 1) Reclassified Recurring Requests by Automation Feasibility First

Client requests were numerous but there were no criteria for which could be standardized as product features.
- Reviewed 70+ hypothesis validation requests one by one, classifying each as dashboard-possible, human-resource-required, or not feasible based on current data and platform capabilities.
- Used repeatability and automation feasibility as the most important classification criteria.
- Instead of accepting client demands as-is, first selected only those convertible to product structure.

This created clear criteria for what could and couldn't be templateized, establishing a starting point for subsequent planning and resource allocation.

### 2) Defined Product Scope by Distinguishing Recognition from Analysis

What clients called "analysis" mixed questions where simple visualization sufficed with questions requiring interpretation and recommendations.
- Rather than dividing requests merely by topic, first separated them into two tiers: recognition and analysis.
- Recognition was defined as questions deliverable repeatedly via dashboard widgets; analysis was separated as the domain requiring analyst interpretation and conclusions.
- This distinction clarified the boundary between problems dashboards solve and problems requiring separate analysis reports.

Reduced excessive expectations for dashboards and enabled more consistent client response and internal resource allocation standards.

### 3) Concretized 10 Analysis Topics into 5 Templates

Even after gathering dashboard-possible requests, they couldn't directly translate to screen designs without regrouping into reusable units.
- Organized 22 dashboard-possible requests into 10 analysis topics under 3 categories: customer understanding / spatial analysis / performance measurement.
- Then restructured into screen-level units immediately usable by clients: store performance comparison, visitor flow confirmation, funnel analysis, product association analysis — concretized into 5 template types.
- During this process, maintained the 10 topics as an analysis framework while consolidating overlapping topics at the screen level to reduce template count.

Converted client requests from one-off screens into a reusable analytics delivery system, securing a publishable template structure.

### 4) Supplemented Structural Questions with New Widgets

Structural questions about inter-zone movement, conversion, and store comparison lacked communicative power with existing charts alone.
- Designed new widgets: scatter+quadrant for 2D store performance comparison, funnel for step-by-step dropout, and traffic map for inter-zone flow visualization.
- Particularly for funnel and movement analysis, judged that showing inter-zone transitions alongside step-by-step aggregation was more appropriate, reviewing transition matrix-based formats using store data.
- Didn't just add widgets but designed new information structures matching client questions.

Complemented existing widget limitations while concretizing templates to a level that can actually answer client questions.
