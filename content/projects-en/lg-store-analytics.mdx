## 10. Limitations & Retrospective

This project clearly had achievements, but also clear regrets.

First, I couldn't connect proposed improvements to field experiments.
Suggestions like Dead Zone repositioning or experience zone flow redesign were included in reports, but I hadn't sufficiently reviewed field cooperation beforehand, so they didn't lead to actual A/B testing. Regardless of the proposal's validity, feasibility review of practical operational processes was insufficient.

Second, I couldn't prove business impact all the way to final KPIs.
I confirmed the relationship between space attractiveness and core KPIs, but couldn't verify within the project scope whether it translated to actual store revenue or customer satisfaction. In similar future projects, connecting operational metrics to business metrics should be included earlier in the design.

Third, management complexity grew rapidly as the metric system expanded.
As events and custom metrics increased, there was a growing need to systematically manage which metrics had which definitions and dependencies — a need I felt more strongly in the project's later stages.

What I learned from this experience was clear.
In enterprise analytics, the important thing isn't just interpreting data well. **Translating field language into measurable structures and ensuring those structures are actually used in operational decision-making** matters more. This project was the first case where I performed that role in earnest.

## The following is a detailed case study.
---

## 1. Project Overview

This project materialized the client's vague operational improvement expectations into an actual analysis framework during the LG Electronics offline store PoC phase.
At the time, the client wanted insights to better operate offline stores beyond simply looking at visitor counts or dwell times. However, they hadn't defined 'which metrics to look at' or 'what criteria to use for comparing stores.'

My role was to organize these ambiguous requirements into a measurable KPI structure, then design consultation determination logic, inter-store comparison systems, and benchmarking frameworks on top of it.
Ultimately, this project supported the full adoption of 20 PoC stores and led to an additional 40-store expansion and incorporation into operational metrics.

## 2. Problem Background

mAy-I is an IT startup operating a SaaS platform that collects and analyzes offline space visitor behavior data through CCTV-based video analytics.

LG Electronics was one of the most important early enterprise clients, and simultaneously one with very high expectations.
Most existing clients were satisfied with basic metrics like visitor count, dwell time, and zone visit ratios. LG Electronics, however, wanted to see how they could actually 'improve' offline stores based on data.
Just as online services like Google Analytics improve product structure based on pageviews and conversion funnels, there was an expectation that offline stores should also be able to change operational strategies based on behavioral data.

The problem was that "expectations existed but no analysis framework did."
The PoC was already underway with data accumulating from 20 stores, but the client hadn't presented specific analysis requirements.
Basic metrics alone couldn't answer questions like:
* Why do some stores perform well while others underperform?
* What needs to change for improvement?
* What criteria should be used to compare stores?
* How should operationally important concepts like `consultation` or `space attractiveness` be handled as data?

Ultimately, the core challenge of this project was **translating vague operational expectations into measurable, comparable, and improvable KPI language**.

## 3. My Role

My responsibilities in this project fell into four main areas.

### 3-1. Analysis Framework Definition

I designed which KPIs would represent the operational concepts the client valued.
This wasn't simply adding metrics — I created an analysis structure that distinguished which metrics represent outcomes and which are causal variables.

### 3-2. Consultation Conversion Metric Design

Based on the staff classification model outputs, I designed and implemented rule-based logic to estimate staff-visitor interactions as consultations.

### 3-3. Store Comparison Framework Design

I designed a framework for comparing and typologizing stores based on 7 core metrics.
This included metric structure, visualization methods, and benchmarking criteria.

### 3-4. Report Writing & Presentation

I wrote analysis reports and directly presented key insights at client meetings.
This wasn't simply delivering analysis results — it was proposing how the client should use this data for operational decisions and persuading them toward full adoption.

## 4. How I Structured the Problem

### 4-1. Instead of listing metrics, I created an interpretable structure

From the start, it was clear that basic metrics alone were insufficient.
Visitor count and average dwell time could show current status but couldn't explain how a store should change. What the client wanted wasn't pretty charts but a data framework that could actually improve operations.

So I first divided metrics into two categories:
* **Interpretive variables**: Characteristics that store staff can't directly change, like visitor gender, age group, and group visit ratio
* **Improvement variables**: Metrics that can change based on operational approach, like dwell time, experience zone conversion rate, consultation conversion rate, and space activity

This distinction was important because **separating what explains a store's current state from what can actually be improved enables subsequent strategy proposals**.

The final result was 7 core metrics organized into 3 categories.

### KPI Structure

1. **Space Attractiveness**
   * Experience zone conversion rate
   * Space activity
   * Average dwell time

2. **Core KPIs**
   * Visitor count
   * Consultation conversion rate

3. **Visitor Characteristics**
   * Average age group
   * Group visit ratio

This structure enabled explaining **"what kind of space each store is"** rather than simply ranking stores by a single score.

### 4-2. How I made the abstract concept of "consultation" measurable

At LG Best Shop, the ratio of visitors who actually received **staff consultation** was a more important operational metric than visitor count itself.
The problem was that this metric was being manually tallied by staff after customer interactions.
A video-based system can't hear audio or directly confirm conversation content. How should "consultation" be datarized in this situation?

So I changed the approach. Rather than trying to measure consultation directly, I first defined **what behavioral patterns appear on video when consultation occurs**.
My hypothesis was: "For a consultation to happen..."

* Staff and visitor must move together within a certain distance.
* It must not be a simple passing — the accompaniment must last beyond a certain duration.
* The behavior of browsing products together must appear repeatedly for a significant time.

The AI development team built the staff classification model, and I designed the interaction determination logic using its outputs.
The core criteria were two:

* **Distance condition**: Within approximately n times shoulder width based on bounding box
* **Time condition**: Accompaniment lasting beyond a certain duration

What I learned from this project was that the most important thing in defining KPIs wasn't whether this logic was the most sophisticated definition.
Rather, **whether it was a standard that the client could understand, trust, and actually use as a KPI in operations** mattered more.

I learned that in enterprise analytics, creating operational standards that practitioners can accept is just as important as model sophistication.

### 4-3. How I built the space attractiveness and store comparison framework

### Defining Space Attractiveness

One concept the client frequently mentioned was "Is our store's space attractive?"

To express this abstract concept with data, I centered on **two metrics per space**:

* **Exposure rate**: The proportion of visitors who passed through a given zone
* **Dwell conversion rate**: The proportion of people who passed through a zone and actually stayed

Combining these, I interpreted spaces as:

* **Hot Zone**: High exposure rate and high dwell conversion rate
* **Dead Zone**: Both metrics low

This approach was far more practical than evaluating space with dwell time alone.
In reality, some zones had high foot traffic but no one stayed, while others had low exposure but visitors who entered stayed long. Separating these differences was more effective for finding store improvement points.

<img src="/images/LG HotDeadZone.png" alt="Hot Zone / Dead Zone example" style={{ width: "100%", borderRadius: "8px" }} />

### Why I chose radar charts over K-means

After the 7 KPIs were organized, the next challenge was how to compare 20 stores.
Initially, I explored unsupervised learning-based clustering (K-means, KNN, etc.). The results themselves weren't bad.
However, the outputs of unsupervised learning had clear limitations in explaining "why the typology turned out this way" to the client.

Telling a non-technical decision-maker "this store belongs to cluster 2" has little practical meaning.
What the client needed during the PoC phase was **an understandable comparison framework** rather than algorithmic sophistication.
Radar charts could intuitively show store strengths and weaknesses, making it immediately readable how one store differs from another.

So I ultimately chose a **radar chart-based comparison framework** using the three KPI categories as axes.
This approach was highly explainable, and it was the area that received the best client response during presentations.

<img src="/images/LG clustering_compare.png" alt="Scatter plot vs radar chart comparison" style={{ width: "100%", borderRadius: "8px" }} />

## 7. What I Proposed from the Analysis Results

During the radar chart-based comparison, meaningful patterns emerged.
**Stores with higher space attractiveness tended to also show higher core KPIs.** This pattern provided the first directional answer to the client's most pressing question: **"What should we change to improve KPIs?"**

Rather than ending this discovery as a simple correlation report, I went one step further to connect it to actual operational actions.
For stores with low space attractiveness, I proposed pairing them with **benchmarking targets** — stores that had similar visitor characteristics but higher space attractiveness.

The advantages of this approach were:

* Providing **comparable examples** rather than abstract improvement theories
* Proposing "stores that perform better under similar conditions" rather than just "refer to good stores"
* Enabling the client to understand improvement directions in a form that could lead to **actual operational actions**
